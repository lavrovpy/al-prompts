1. First create a copy deck for the poster and ask me to approve it.

Copy deck requirements:
- Write the poster for learners whose native language is [NATIVE_LANGUAGE] and who are learning [TARGET_LANGUAGE].
- Use natural, high-frequency words and phrases for the selected topic.
- Avoid rare, academic, or overly formal vocabulary unless the topic requires it.
- Use correct grammar, spelling, accents, and diacritics in both languages.
- Keep all text short enough to render clearly in an image.
- Use the [TARGET_LANGUAGE] text first, then the [NATIVE_LANGUAGE] translation.
- Make the [TARGET_LANGUAGE] words visually dominant in the final poster.
- Present the copy deck as the exact final in-image text, grouped by title and section.

Copy deck limits:
- 1 title.
- 4 content sections maximum.
- 5 vocabulary items per section maximum.
- 1 short useful phrase or sentence per section maximum.
- No paragraphs.
- Prioritize legibility and correctness over completeness.

Title:
- Create a clear, natural title entirely in [TARGET_LANGUAGE].
- Base it on the selected topic.
- Do not use an English scaffold such as "Vocabulary:" unless English is the target language.
- Keep it short enough to fit on one line when possible.

Image style:
Use a polished language-book / textbook infographic style. Combine common vocabulary, simple categories, useful phrases, short example sentences, and clear visual associations. Make it look like a modern illustrated classroom poster or study cheat sheet.

Canvas and layout:
- Use a clean 16:9 landscape layout.
- Reserve a dedicated top title area across the full width of the image.
- Keep the title area clearly separated from the content area.
- Leave generous empty space below the title before any section boxes begin.
- Keep all content sections completely below the title area.
- Use safe margins around the entire image.
- Arrange the content sections in a balanced grid of rounded, color-coded boxes.
- Use small illustrations and icons next to words and phrases.
- Avoid one huge central illustration that takes over the page.
- Use only one large main title at the top.

Typography and legibility:
- Use readable typography with strong contrast.
- The title should be large and bold, but must fit comfortably inside the reserved top title area.
- If the title is long, reduce the title size slightly instead of letting it overlap the content.
- Make the [TARGET_LANGUAGE] words bold and visually dominant.
- Make the [NATIVE_LANGUAGE] translations smaller but still easy to read.
- Leave enough spacing so section titles, vocabulary items, translations, and phrases do not overlap.
- Avoid tiny text.
- Do not crop any text.

Visual design:
- Use bright but clean colors.
- Use flat-vector or soft cartoon illustrations.
- Use icons, small character illustrations, arrows, dots, dividers, and callout lines where helpful.
- Keep the layout balanced, uncluttered, and easy to study.
- Make the final image feel like a polished illustrated vocabulary page from a modern language-learning book.

Omit:
subtitles, taglines, watermarks, unrelated logos, random extra text, misspellings, large decorative center scenes, title-content collisions, cropped text, overlapping section boxes, irrelevant words, and language mix-ups.
